01 — For readers new to the project

Who was Ionesco, and what is Rhinoceros?

The earlier version of this project assumed that readers already knew the author and the play. This revised version begins with basic context so that a non-specialist reader can follow the argument.

Eugène Ionesco

Romanian-born French dramatist Eugène Ionesco was born in Slatina, Romania in 1909 and died in Paris in 1994. Britannica describes him as a major twentieth-century dramatist associated with the Theatre of the Absurd; his early “antiplay” The Bald Soprano helped transform modern drama, and he was elected to the Académie Française in 1970.

Britannica bio ↗

The play

Rhinoceros is a three-act, quasi-allegorical play. It was produced in Germany in 1959 and published in French the same year as Le Rhinocéros. In the play, the inhabitants of a small town gradually turn into rhinoceroses; the ordinary and flawed Bérenger becomes the last figure who resists the transformation.

Britannica play entry ↗

Why it matters

The plot is simple enough to be memorable, but flexible enough to be historically reactivated. “Turning into rhinoceroses” can be read as fascist mass conversion, social conformity, ideological contagion, dehumanization, or the pressure to abandon individual judgment.

02 — Concept

What does “reception history” mean here?

Reception history means the history of how a work is received, described, interpreted, taught, reviewed, staged, marketed, and reused by later readers, critics, publishers, and theatre communities. In this project, I am not only studying what Ionesco wrote. I am studying how later communities have explained what the play means.

Theoretical frame

The project is loosely informed by reception theory, especially the idea that meaning changes as works meet different historical “horizons of expectation.” In practical terms, I treat reviews, performance notes, publisher descriptions, and academic articles as evidence of changing interpretive communities.

Jauss, Toward an Aesthetic of Reception ↗Reception theory overview ↗

My position

The point is not that later readings are “more correct.” The point is that the same play becomes useful for different moments. A digital humanities approach can make that movement visible by turning scattered reception materials into a structured, inspectable corpus.

03 — Research Question and Humanistic Significance

What new knowledge does this project try to produce?

How has the reception of Ionesco's Rhinoceros changed over time, and does the language of reception move from fascism/totalitarianism toward broader concerns such as conformity, herd mentality, identity loss, and dehumanization?

Historical meaning

The project asks how twentieth-century anti-fascist memory is preserved, softened, or generalized in later cultural contexts.

Humanistic meaning

It shows that literary meaning is not fixed inside the text alone. Meaning is produced again whenever readers use the work to name their own political and social anxieties.

Digital meaning

It demonstrates how metadata, coding, visualization, and LLM-assisted classification can make interpretive change more visible without replacing close reading.

04 — Scope, Geography, and Periodization

What exactly is being compared?

The project now uses an expanded corpus to reduce the 1962–2020 gap. The periodization still remains a working structure, not a complete global reception history, but the middle decades are no longer treated as an empty interval.

Geographic scope

Current scope: primarily English-language reception from the United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, and educational/publisher contexts, supplemented by French and Israeli reception markers. This means the current website still does not claim to represent global reception as a whole.

Next expansion: more French, German, Hebrew, Korean, and Romanian reception should be added before making broader claims.

Periodization criteria

The four periods are based on production history, available reception records, and shifts in interpretive vocabulary. They are analytical scaffolds: 1959–1975 captures early stage/film circulation, 1976–2000 captures canonization and limited digitized traces, 2001–2016 captures renewed scholarly and digital-theatre circulation, and 2017–2026 captures explicitly contemporary political resonance.

1959–1975

Premiere & early Anglophone reception

Initial stage circulation, Broadway reception, Cold War context, absurdist form, anti-fascist allegory.

1976–2000

Canonization and intellectual reframing

Film adaptation, author interviews, and academic/theatre reception reframe the play through nonconformity, ideological contagion, and the absurdist canon. The period remains under-digitized but is now represented.

2001–2016

Scholarly and digital-theatre circulation

Royal Court revival, journal articles, festival reviews, and online theatre criticism make conformity, identity, kitsch, and performance style increasingly explicit.

2017–2026

Contemporary political resonance

Recent reviews connect the play to conformity, authoritarianism, contagion, herd mentality, identity, and dehumanization.

05 — Data and Coded Sources

What does “coded source” mean?

A coded source is one reception item that has been turned into structured data. For each source I record metadata and interpretive tags, then compare those tags across time and source type.

Metadata

year, period, region, source type, title, publication, URL

Interpretive frame

fascism, conformity, identity/dehumanization, absurdism, contemporary resonance, staging

Evidence

keywords, phrases, short summaries, and close-reading notes

Criticism

genre bias, political orientation, language bias, and access limits

Remaining limits: It does not eliminate source imbalance: recent English-language digital materials are still much easier to access than older print reviews, non-English criticism, and paywalled archives.
06 — Methodology

How are the interpretive shifts measured?

The revised site makes the measurement criteria explicit: interpretive shifts are not measured by a single word cloud. They are triangulated through source metadata, manual coding, keyword groups, LLM-assisted semantic classification, and close reading.

Step 1

Collect

Gather reviews, descriptions, study materials, and scholarship.

Step 2

Code

Assign source type, region, period, and candidate frames.

Step 3

Count

Track keyword groups, not isolated words.

Step 4

Classify

Use LLM API as a coding assistant for semantic frame scores.

Step 5

Interpret

Use close reading to explain what the numbers cannot.

Frame score definition

A frame score is a 0–1 estimate of how strongly a reception text fits a predefined interpretive frame. It is derived from manual tags, keyword evidence, or LLM classification. It is not a claim about the “true” meaning of the play.

AI usage disclosure

Project planning and coding were assisted by ChatGPT. The AI Lab can call external LLM APIs through a secure backend endpoint: GPT/OpenAI, Claude/Anthropic, or Gemini/Google, depending on which API keys are configured. Demo mode uses precomputed examples. AI is used as a coding assistant, not an objective judge.

09 — Revised Argument

What is my own argument?

My argument is that Rhinoceros remains culturally powerful because it is both historically anchored and structurally portable. Its anti-fascist origin matters, but later reception shows how that origin is extended into a broader vocabulary for conformity, collective irrationality, social contagion, and the loss of human individuality.

What readers learn

Readers learn how a literary work travels through institutions: theatre reviews, classrooms, publisher summaries, scholarly articles, and AI classification systems. The project therefore shows not only a play's meaning but the social life of interpretation.

What DH adds

Digital methods do not replace interpretation. They make a scattered reception history inspectable: readers can see where the data is strong, where it is weak, and how AI classifications agree or diverge from human-coded frames.
