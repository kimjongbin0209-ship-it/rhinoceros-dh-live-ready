A Digital Humanities Analysis of the Reception of Eugène Ionesco's Rhinoceros

This project traces how Rhinoceros has been read not only as an anti-fascist allegory, but increasingly as a broader critique of conformity, herd mentality, identity loss, and dehumanization.

01 — Research Question

How did Rhinoceros move from an anti-fascist allegory to a broader critique of conformity?

"How has the reception of Eugène Ionesco's Rhinoceros changed over time? Was the play initially framed primarily as an anti-fascist or anti-totalitarian allegory, and has its reception gradually expanded toward broader concerns such as conformity, herd mentality, mass psychology, identity, and dehumanization?"

Which interpretive keywords appear in different periods, and how do their frequencies shift across the timeline of reception?

How do political readings relating to fascism and totalitarianism relate to broader social or psychological readings?

What does this reception history reveal about the changing meanings of literary texts in relation to their historical contexts?

02 — Methodology

From Corpus Building to Close Reading

The project combines digital text analysis with traditional hermeneutics.

Step 01

Corpus Building

Theatre reviews, academic articles, publisher descriptions, and university performance programmes were gathered from databases, newspaper archives, and digital repositories.

Step 02

Metadata Collection

Each source was tagged with year, period, source type, publication/organization, and language. This structured metadata enables period-based and type-based filtering.

Step 03

Text Cleaning

Texts were normalized: punctuation standardized, stopwords removed, and variant spellings of key terms (e.g. "rhinoceritis", "rhinocerisation") unified for consistent counting.

Step 04

Keyword Grouping

Interpretive terms were clustered into thematic frames: fascism/totalitarianism, conformity/herd mentality, identity/dehumanization, absurdism/satire, and contemporary resonance.

Step 05

Preliminary Manual Coding

The current dataset of 12 sources was coded by hand, assigning dominant interpretive frames. All visualizations based on this stage are explicitly labeled as preliminary.

Step 06

Keyword Frequency Analysis

Planned for the next stage: automated frequency counts across the full corpus will replace and supplement the manual coding shown here.

Step 07

Close Reading

Quantitative findings are interpreted through close reading of selected sources, ensuring that statistical patterns are grounded in textual evidence and historical context.

06 — Close Reading Evidence

Three Interpretive Frames in the Corpus

Selected examples from the corpus illustrate how different source types and historical periods activate different interpretive frameworks.

Early Reception · 1961

Farce, Satire, and the Broadway Stage

Time magazine's 1961 review of the Broadway production — starring Zero Mostel and directed by Joseph Anthony — foregrounds farce, satire, rationalizations, and what the play calls "rhinoceros fever." The explicit anti-fascist allegory is present but embedded in a discussion of comic performance and theatrical spectacle.

The Playbill record of the same production contextualizes Zero Mostel's Tony-winning performance, illustrating how early American reception shaped the play's popular meaning through theatrical celebrity and spectacle rather than political exegesis.

farce satire rationalization rhinoceros fever Broadway Zero Mostel
Academic-Political Frame · 2017–2021

Fascism, the Iron Guard, and Loss of Individuality

Academic sources such as the 2021 article in Turkish Academic Research Review ground the play firmly in its Romanian biographical context: Ionesco's experience of watching intellectuals join the fascist Iron Guard in the 1930s. This frame treats the "rhinocerisation process" as a systematic analysis of totalitarian ideology and individual capitulation.

The 2017 People's World review similarly invokes fascism, xenophobia, and political warning — though its specific political orientation requires critical source evaluation.

fascism Iron Guard totalitarianism rhinocerisation loss of individuality Romania
Contemporary Frame · 2024–2025

Conformity, Contagion, and Modern Resonance

The 2025 Observer review explicitly contrasts older Nazi/East European authoritarian readings with what it calls "a broader attack on unreflecting conformism." The 2024 Le Monde review of a French production describes a staging that strips away explicit historical references to emphasize epidemic, contamination, and community threat as social metaphors.

Together, these contemporary sources demonstrate a recognizable drift in critical framing: the political specificity of the original allegory is supplemented — and sometimes displaced — by a more generalized psychology of collective irrationality.

conformity herd mentality epidemic / contagion dehumanization authoritarianism contemporary resonance
07 — Data Criticism

Methodological Limitations & Honest Reflection

A responsible digital humanities project must be transparent about what its data cannot tell us.

Archive Access Bias

Older reviews from 1959–1975 are significantly harder to access digitally. The corpus is therefore skewed toward recent sources, which may exaggerate the apparent novelty of contemporary frames.

Uneven Source Types

The dataset mixes theatre reviews, academic articles, publisher descriptions, and university notices. These genres use interpretive language differently and for different audiences, making direct keyword comparison problematic.

Publisher vs. Academic Language

Licensing and publisher descriptions (e.g. Concord Theatricals) are written for marketing, not analysis. Their keyword choices reflect commercial framing rather than scholarly interpretation.

English-Language Bias

The corpus is predominantly English-language. French, German, and other-language reception may weight political and aesthetic frames differently. The 2024 Le Monde source is a notable but insufficient counterweight.

Keyword vs. Close Reading

Keyword frequency alone cannot distinguish between a critic who mentions fascism to reject that reading and one who endorses it. Close reading is an essential complement to any quantitative analysis.

Sentiment Analysis Limits

Sentiment analysis is secondary for this project because theatre reviews primarily judge productions rather than articulating interpretive positions. A positive review may still contain negative framing of the play's themes.

09 — Conclusion

A Structurally Flexible Allegory

Rhinoceros remains powerful because its allegory is both historically specific and structurally flexible. It can be read through the memory of fascism and totalitarianism, but it also continues to describe broader patterns of conformity, collective irrationality, identity loss, and dehumanization.

This reception history suggests that the play's meaning is not simply discovered but actively constructed by each historical moment. The dominance of conformity and herd-mentality framing in recent criticism is not a correction of earlier fascist readings but an extension of them — a sign that the mechanisms Ionesco dramatized remain legible, and urgent, in very different political contexts.

Further stages of this project will expand the corpus, conduct systematic keyword-frequency analysis, and incorporate non-English-language sources to test whether this preliminary pattern holds across a broader and more balanced dataset.
